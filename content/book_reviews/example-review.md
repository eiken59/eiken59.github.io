In economics, people are usually assumed to be rational—making consistent and logical choices. However, Kahneman showed that this is rarely the case.

Our brain is a machine with limited computing power, shaped by historical biases and full of "bugs." Kahneman uses System 1 and System 2 to explain how we process information. For example, when "something feels off," it might seem irrational. However, Kahneman explains this biologically: our genes wired us to sense potential threats in a microsecond. Knowing this, we shouldn't worry too much about why we feel a certain way. Instead, we should use your logical brain (System 2) to check if the alarm raised by our intuitive brain (System 1) is actually real.

To me, this book feels more about economics than psychology. In psychology, people often argue about whether our past or our future goals shape us more. 

Kahneman doesn't pick a side. Instead, he reminds us that statistics explain reality better than these ideologies. For example, someone with very few resources and a trust fund baby have completely different "base rates" for success. Because of this gap, the rewards they get for succeeding are also vastly different.

Given this harsh reality, if a person with few resources chooses to give up, are they just being cowardly? Not necessarily. It might actually be a highly rational decision based on calculating their expected return. Before judging someone as too pessimistic or optimistic, we should first look at their base rate.

This same idea applies to "regression to the mean." The world is full of noise and randomness, making long-term predictions unreliable. For instance, a smart child won't necessarily perform brilliantly all the time. Many successes or failures aren't caused by a specific reason; they just happen due to chance.

In the end, Kahneman doesn't teach us how to completely eliminate bias. Instead, he teaches us to recognize it and become more self-aware. To me, true rationality means understanding my own situation clearly and learning to properly evaluate the sudden alarms triggered by my System 1.
